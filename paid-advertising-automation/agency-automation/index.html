Agency automation — scale accounts without scaling headcount.

The economics of a traditional agency are brutal: every new client account means more optimisation hours, and more hours means another hire. Automation breaks that curve. Here is what to automate first, how multi-tenant guardrails keep each client safe, and how white-label lets the AI work underneath your brand.

The agency margin problem

In a manual agency, headcount scales linearly with accounts. Each new client brings the same recurring grind — bid reviews, budget pacing, search-term cleanup, weekly reporting — and a finite number of accounts an account manager can hold before quality drops. Add ten clients, add staff. Margins stay flat at best, and the people you hire to grow are the same cost that caps your growth.

Automation breaks the linear curve. When the recurring optimisation cadence runs autonomously inside rules your team defined, one account manager can supervise many more accounts — not by working faster, but by spending their time on the work that actually compounds. The headcount line flattens while the account line keeps climbing. That gap is your margin.

The point is not to replace your people. It is to stop spending their hours on tasks a guardrail-driven agent does the same way every week, and redirect those hours to strategy and client relationships — the things clients actually pay an agency for.

What to automate first — and what stays human

Not everything should be automated, and not everything should be automated at once. Start with the high-frequency, rules-based work that eats the most hours across your account base:

  • Reporting: recurring client reports assembled from account data on a fixed schedule, in your format, without an analyst rebuilding them by hand every week
  • Recurring audits: a standing checklist run against every account — disapproved ads, broken tracking, budget overruns, missing negatives — surfaced before the client notices
  • Search-term and negative workflows: flagging wasteful search terms and proposing negative keyword additions within guardrails, instead of a manual sweep per account per week
  • Budget reallocation: shifting spend from underperformers to top converters inside rate limits your team sets, rather than waiting for a monthly manual review
  • Brief generation: first-draft creative and campaign briefs assembled from account context, ready for a strategist to refine

What stays human is the work that makes you an agency rather than a tool: strategy and the client relationship. Positioning, channel mix, the call on whether a campaign is even the right move, and the trust you hold with the client — those are not guardrail problems. The model is human-in-the-loop: the agent does the recurring execution, your people own the judgement and the conversation.

Multi-tenant with per-client guardrails

Running many accounts through one automation layer only works if each client is fully walled off from the next. The platform is multi-tenant: every client gets their own constraints, their own decision logs, and their own dashboard. A guardrail you set for one client never leaks into another, and the audit trail for each account stands on its own.

That means you can run aggressive reallocation rules for a client who wants growth and conservative caps for a client who wants protection — at the same time, in the same platform, without the two ever touching. Every action an agent takes is constrained by that client's rules and written to that client's log with the trigger, the action, and the measured impact.

Example per-client guardrail set
Daily spend cap per campaign

Human gate

Each client's set is tuned to their risk tolerance and their account. The dashboard shows you every account at a glance, and the per-client log gives you — and the client — a complete record of what the automation did and why.

White-label — your brand on top, the AI underneath

To your clients, it is your agency doing the work. White-label means the dashboard, the reports, and the client-facing surface carry your brand — not ours. The AI runs underneath, inside the guardrails you configured, and the client experiences it as your team's capability.

This matters for two reasons. The first is trust: clients hire your agency, and the relationship stays with you. They never need to know which engine sits behind the optimisation cadence, only that their accounts are well run and their reports arrive on time. The second is data control: client data is segmented by tenant, with per-client logs that you and the client can audit. Nothing is co-mingled, and you stay in control of what each client sees.

The result is leverage without exposure. You scale the operation behind the scenes while the client sees a tighter, more responsive agency.
