Workflow Automation

AI consulting services — when you need one, and how not to get burned.

An AI marketing-automation consultant should leave you with a running system you own and the ability to operate it without them. Most leave you with a slide deck and a retainer. This is a buyer's guide to telling the difference — written, candidly, by people who do this work.

When to hire one — and when not to

Outside help is worth it when the problem is knowledge or velocity, not when it is a thing you could learn in a week. The honest signals that you need a consultant:

  • You know automation would help, but no one on the team has built a guardrailed, human-in-the-loop system before and you can't afford to learn on production campaigns.
  • You have the tools — a CRM, an ad platform, maybe an LLM subscription — and none of them talk to each other in any reliable way.
  • Manual work is the bottleneck: the same reporting, asset, or QA tasks eat hours every week and scale linearly with revenue.
  • You need it shipped in weeks, not the two or three quarters an internal hire would take to ramp.

And the signals you should not hire one — at least not yet:

  • Your data is a mess and your processes are undocumented. Automating a broken foundation just makes the breakage faster and harder to see. Fix the foundation first.
  • You have a capable, curious team and time to upskill. In that case buy training, not a build.
  • You can't yet name the workflow you want automated. If you can't describe the manual version in steps, no consultant can automate it for you.

If you're unsure which camp you're in, that's exactly what the readiness check below is for.

What a good engagement actually produces

The deliverable is not a strategy document. A good AI marketing-automation engagement ends with things that keep working after the consultant logs off for the last time:

  • Running systems. Workflows that execute on a schedule or trigger, inside guardrails your team defined, with a human in the loop where judgment matters.
  • Documentation. How each workflow is wired, what every rule does, and how to change it — written for your team, not locked in someone's head.
  • Owned IP. The prompts, the configurations, the logic — all of it is yours. It lives in your accounts, under your credentials.
  • Training. Your people can operate, extend, and turn off what was built without filing a support ticket.

This is the part worth being blunt about: the goal of a good engagement is to make itself unnecessary. You own the system, there is no lock-in, and the relationship ends with you independent. A consultant whose business model depends on you never learning how their work functions is not selling you automation — they're selling you a dependency. Our Automation Sprints are built this way on purpose: two to four weeks, client owns the output, no retainer.

Red flags

Most of the ways an AI consulting engagement goes wrong are visible in the sales conversation, if you know what to listen for.

Black-box tools

If you can't see what a workflow does, why it fired, or how to change a rule, you can't trust it and you can't fix it. Opaque automation is a liability dressed up as a feature.

Vendor lock-in

Watch for work that only runs on the consultant's proprietary platform, lives in their accounts, or stops the day you stop paying. If you can't take it with you, you don't own it.

The "platform" upsell

A discovery call that quietly turns into a pitch for a seat-based monthly product you didn't ask about is a sign the engagement is a customer-acquisition funnel, not a solution to your problem.

No handover

If the proposal has no documentation, no training, and no plan for what happens when they leave, ask why. The answer is usually that leaving was never the plan.

Automation before your foundation is ready

A consultant eager to automate before looking hard at your data quality and processes is optimising for their invoice, not your outcome. A good one will tell you when you're not ready — even if it costs them the deal.

How to evaluate one

Push past the deck and ask questions that surface how an engagement actually ends. Good consultants answer these plainly; the ones to avoid get vague.

Evaluation checklist
Who owns the IP, prompts, and configs when we're done?
Ownership
Does this run in our accounts, or only on your platform?
Lock-in
What documentation and training do we get at handover?
Handover
Where is the human in the loop, and what are the guardrails?
Control
Can we operate and turn this off without you?
Independence
When would you tell us NOT to automate something?
Honesty

The last question is the most useful one. A consultant who can't name a situation where automation is the wrong call will automate anything you'll pay for. You can read how we think about all of this on our about page.
