AI automation or another agency retainer? How to choose

The question usually arrives framed as a fight: do you trust software to run your campaigns, or do you hire people who know what they are doing? That framing is wrong, and it leads to bad decisions. The real split is between the work that needs a human brain and the work that just needs something watching the account at 2 a.m. Get that division right and the AI-or-agency question mostly answers itself.

Why "AI versus agency" is the wrong question

When you phrase it as AI versus people, you force a choice that does not exist in practice. An agency does not replace automation, and automation does not replace strategy, positioning, creative judgment, or knowing why a campaign is underperforming. Those are human jobs and they stay human jobs.

What you are actually deciding is who handles the watch-and-act work: the constant, low-glamour monitoring of budgets, bids, pacing, and anomalies that eats hours and rarely needs a strategist's depth. That work is where retainers quietly bleed value, because a senior person ends up babysitting dashboards instead of thinking. It is also exactly the work bounded automation is built to absorb.

Split the work before you compare the options

Before you price an agency or a platform, separate your marketing work into two buckets. One bucket is judgment: strategy, offer design, creative, channel mix, the calls that depend on context only a person has. The other bucket is execution depth: the repetitive monitoring and adjustment that follows clear rules once someone sets them.

  • Judgment work — what to say, to whom, and why. This belongs to people, whether in-house or an agency.
  • Watch-and-act work — catch the spend spike, pause the broken ad, shift budget to the line that is converting. This is automatable, and automating it frees your people for the first bucket.

Most teams discover the buckets are tangled. The execution depth spectrum is a useful way to see where a given task actually sits, and it tends to reveal that you do not need more people for the second bucket. You need something dependable watching it.

The real risk is not automation. It is the black box.

Plenty of buyers have been burned by automation that spent money and could not explain itself. That is a legitimate fear, but the problem was never that software acted. The problem was that it acted without limits and without a record. The enemy is the black box, not the act of automating.

This is where Trigger, Action, Impact matters. Every automated change should be three visible things: a trigger (a condition you chose to watch), an action (a specific change made through a real, auditable connection), and a measured impact you can read after the fact. If a change cannot be named, traced, and reversed, it should not happen. Automation done this way is more accountable than a human quietly tweaking bids on a Friday afternoon, because nothing is hidden.

It also means the tool connects to your account with read-only, auditable access first, and acts only inside guardrails you set — spend caps, change ceilings, exclusions, approval thresholds. You decide the bounds. The automation works inside them, and you can revoke the connection at any time.

When an agency still earns its retainer

Hire people, or keep your agency, for the judgment bucket. If you need a brand repositioned, a new market entered, a campaign concept that did not exist yesterday, or a partner to argue with you about strategy, that is human work and worth paying for. No automation does that, and any tool that claims to is selling you the black box.

The mistake is paying a retainer rate for watch-and-act work. When a chunk of your monthly fee covers someone monitoring pacing and nudging budgets, you are buying expensive attention for a job that bounded automation handles continuously and transparently. The better arrangement is people on strategy, automation on the watch — and the two reinforcing each other rather than competing.

How to make the call

Decide in this order. First, list what genuinely needs human judgment and protect that work — staff it or keep your agency for it. Second, identify the watch-and-act tasks draining hours and ask whether they could run inside guardrails with every action visible and reversible. Third, start small and bounded rather than handing over the whole account.
