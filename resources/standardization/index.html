Campaign Standardization.

Automation scripts need consistent structure to know what to act on. An ad group named "Test 3 - Dave - old" can't be automated reliably. A naming convention can.

Naming conventionsLaunch SOPsQA templatesCampaign structure
📊 Readiness Score weight: 20% — tied second with instrumentation and governance

Why inconsistent structure breaks automation

Automation rules, scripts, and AI workflows operate on pattern matching. A Google Ads script that's supposed to pause underperforming ad groups needs to identify those ad groups reliably. A budget pacing rule that fires on campaigns above a spend threshold needs to know which campaigns qualify. A reporting workflow that breaks performance down by channel needs consistent signals to segment correctly.

When campaign names are ad hoc — "Test", "Dave's retargeting", "Q3 promo v2 FINAL" — automation can't parse what it's acting on. Rules misfire. Reports segment incorrectly. Alerts trigger on the wrong campaigns. The fix isn't more sophisticated automation. It's structure.

Standardization isn't bureaucracy. It's the foundation that makes every automation layer above it more reliable. A team with a consistent naming convention gets 2× more value from the same automation ruleset than a team without one.

Building a naming convention that sticks

A naming convention works when it's both complete (covers everything that matters) and simple (easy to follow without looking it up). Most conventions that fail are too detailed — 15-segment names that no one can remember.

Recommended naming structure

Use 5–6 segments separated by underscores or dashes. Include enough context to filter and segment in reports without needing to open the campaign.

[Channel]_[Objective]_[Audience]_[Creative type]_[Date]

Examples:
GA_CONV_Retargeting_Responsive_2025Q3
META_AWARE_Prospecting_Video_2025Q3
GA_TRAF_Branded_Text_2025Q3

Build the convention into your launch template. The path of least resistance should be the right one — if the naming convention is part of the document you open to start a campaign, it gets followed.

The launch template

A launch template is a checklist that every campaign passes through before it goes live. It doesn't need to be sophisticated — a Google Sheet with checkboxes is sufficient. It needs to include:

  • Campaign naming verified against convention
  • Budget and pacing settings confirmed
  • Conversion actions mapped correctly
  • Audiences verified (inclusions and exclusions)
  • Creative assets checked (sizes, character limits, CTAs)
  • UTM parameters applied correctly
  • QA reviewer sign-off (even if it's the same person)
